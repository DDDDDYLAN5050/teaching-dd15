Is Internet Inclusive?
A visualization about Internet access around the world

We analysed data from 100 countries to provide an overview of how the Internet is accessed and who is behind the computer around the world. We aim to provide a global point of view and, also, show peculiarities from each country. Do you know that despite having a mere 60% of Internet penetration, the average internet user in the Philippines spends more than 10 hours online? And that data shows that countries with higher GDPs, although having high penetration rates, spend less time online? How come some countries with low literacy rates spend so much time online? Is there a correlation? These are the kinds of questions we believe viewers may raise when experiencing our visualization.

Our databases comprehend the penetration of the Internet in various countries around the world, correlating variables like literacy, gender disparity among the amount of internet users, time spent online, trust on non-governmental websites and GDP per capita. Data is visually presented in an arc, resembling a kind of world parliament of internet and enabling the audience to easily compare all variables. In the end, this allows for an analysis of how inclusive is the internet worldwide.

The process
How we arrived here?

During the design process we experimented various ways of portraying the data. We decided to use an arc due to its decent coverage of the available space and the possibility to compare data among countries. The shape is also visually appealing as it relates to the idea of a parliament, reinforcing the idea of a strong inter-relation between countries. The use of colours in the graph helps to distinguish countries by continent and to enhance contrast between them.

A few of the sketches tried out during the process.

How to read the visualization
Into the details

Annotations:We added notes to the graph that comment or highlight interesting facts related to the countries.

Figures: Time spent online is visualized with arcs inside circles, resembling clocks; GDP is represented by rhombuses that vary their area according to the data; Internet Penetration is shown in % with bars; Gender gap is a comparison of two lines expressing percentages; Literacy is a percentual value portrayed by small books and, lastly, the Trust in non-governmental websites is again percentual but represented with circles in varying positions.

The direction: The visualisation can be read from through the arc from left to right and from outside to inside. In the first case, data will is read by country whereas in the second, by each of the datasets represented.

Colors: Africa in orange, America in blue, Asia in red, Europe in green and Oceania in purple. Hues were vary in saturation and luminosity among each group of data displayed.

The Internet Penetration
With the GDP and the Time Spent Online

Two important values are the Internet Penetration per % of population and the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita (USD) - a good way to track the health of a nation's economy, and the Time Spent Online.

We can see the relations between values: more GDP brings more Internet Penetration. And in the same continent one can see huge differences between countries. Also, there is a relation on the Time Spent Online, measured in hours and minutes. In this case it is interesting to note that countries with smaller GDP per capita and Internet Penetration spent more time online like Brazil and Philippines.

Interesting fact: in Asia we see a huge contrast between countries, one can find Qatar as one of the most connected countries, meanwhile Pakistan is in the opposite position.

This part of the graph shows the Leyend, the Time Spent Online, Internet Penetration and GDP per capita in more detail.

Other variables
Gender, Trust in non-government websites and literacy

The percentages of men and women internet users is shown by country, related respectively to the male and female population of those countries. African states show higher percentages of men using the internet, while Europe presents a more balanced comparison.

Interesting facts: in Philippines, a country with small penetration rates and GDP/capita, women use the Internet more than men. Meanwhile, in Europe, the continent with the highest GDPs per capita, only Ireland has more women than men using the Internet.

We can also find contrasts relating the Literacy of the total population (%) of a country and the Trust in non-governmental websites. Generally countries with less literacy trust more in the non-governmental websites and those with higher literacy rates trust less.

Interesting facts: in Pakistan, the level of literacy is low in comparison with other Asian countries but the trust in non-government websites is also very little. On the other hand, the Philippines has a high literacy level yet also presents high levels of trust.
